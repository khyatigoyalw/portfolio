How I launched a brand from zero

The unglamorous first 90 days — name, package, first customer, first repeat. Notes from taking CUPEESE from a blank page to real revenue.

There was no brand. Just a category problem.

When I started on CUPEESE, there was no logo, no name, no packaging — just a premium cheesecake product that looked, on paper, like every other dessert brand in a crowded category. Cakes, pastries and cheesecakes all tend to look the same on a shelf or a delivery app. Before I could think about a single creative asset, I had to solve a category problem: what would make someone remember this dessert five minutes after they finished it?

The idea came before the identity

Instead of starting with a logo, I started with the product experience. Traditional cheesecake slices need a plate, cutlery, careful handling — none of which survives a delivery bag or a work-from-café afternoon. That observation became the whole brand: “Cheesecake in a Cup.” A transparent cup that showed off the layers, was easy to carry, easy to eat one-handed, and looked good on a phone screen before it was ever tasted. The packaging wasn't decoration on top of the product — it was the product.

Naming, identity, and the discipline of "no"

CUPEESE — cup + please — had to do a lot of work in one word: playful, easy to say, easy to own. Every decision after that, from the colour palette to the outer box, got tested against one question: does this feel indulgent without feeling like it's trying too hard? A lot of good ideas got cut because they answered a different brief than the one the brand actually needed.

Shipping before it felt ready

0→1 doesn't reward perfectionism. I took the concept through naming, identity, packaging and positioning, then ran the actual launch — pricing, channel selection, the first batch of creative, the first cohort of customers. There wasn't a big-budget campaign behind it. There was a product people wanted to photograph, a story that was easy to repeat, and a go-to-market plan sized to the brand's actual stage, not an aspirational one.

What ₹8L+ in 90 days actually taught me

The brand crossed ₹8L+ in its first 90 days, but the number that mattered more to me was repeat orders — proof that people weren't just buying the novelty once. Building something from zero means every early customer is also market research. I watched what people actually said about the cup format, not what I assumed they'd say, and used that to sharpen the packaging copy and the reorder messaging in the following weeks.

Launching from zero isn't about having more resources than the category leaders — it's about being more specific than they are. CUPEESE won its first ninety days by being unmistakably itself, not by being louder.
