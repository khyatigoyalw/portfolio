Marketing lessons from FMCG

What selling honey, ghee, superfoods and cheesecake across three very different brands taught me about repeat purchase, retention, and creative that actually converts.

Packaging doesn’t end at the unboxing

Premium food products are usually differentiated through ingredients and branding, but most brands stop thinking about packaging the moment the box is opened. When we developed packaging for Naturally Good's Pure Raw Honey and A2 Bilona Cow Ghee, we added a complimentary wooden honey dipper and a dedicated wooden spoon — small additions that solved a real usage problem and meant the packaging kept adding value every single time the customer used the product, not just once at unboxing.

A new range needs an ecosystem, not just new SKUs

Launching the Superfoods range taught me that a product launch isn't really about the product — it's about making a shelf full of new SKUs feel like they already belong to the brand family. Every product had to look and feel consistent, and go-to-market had to be integrated across packaging, product photography, Amazon listings, website content and social, rather than treating each new item as its own mini-launch.

One creative strategy doesn’t work across brands

Running performance creative across three brands in the same food and beverage category — Naturally Good, HIOC and Cupeese — made it obvious that customers buy the same category for completely different reasons. A Naturally Good customer is investing in long-term health through a subscription. An HIOC customer is making a spontaneous, craving-driven café decision. A Cupeese customer is buying indulgence, celebration, or a gift. Copying one brand's ad formula onto another's audience is the fastest way to waste a media budget.

Retention is a design decision, not a follow-up email

Across FMCG and D2C alike, the products that earned repeat purchase were the ones where retention was designed in from the packaging stage — a reason to keep the container, a reason to reorder, a small delight that made the second purchase easier to justify than the first. Growth dashboards across all three brands made this visible: the campaigns that looked good on reach were rarely the ones that moved repeat-purchase rate, and the packaging-level decisions often outperformed the media-level ones.

FMCG rewards brands that treat every touchpoint — the packaging, the ad, the follow-up message — as part of one connected customer relationship, not as separate line items on a media plan.
